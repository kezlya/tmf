# TMF Origin Story

Use this as the foundation for LinkedIn posts, YouTube videos, landing page copy, and Skool content.

---

## The Short Version (LinkedIn post length)

I'm 40, I have four kids, a new house with $5K/month payments, and I run multiple businesses. Every week I wrote 10-20 tasks on sticky notes. The same tasks. Week after week. Three months of sticky notes piled up on my desk. Many were duplicates. I kept rewriting "leaderboard" and "deduplication strategy" because they never got done — and writing them down didn't empty my head.

I was living paycheck to paycheck on $250K/year because I couldn't tell where my time actually went. I felt like I was always working. But was I working on income-generating tasks, or was I spending all my time on side projects?

Then I set up an AI assistant using OpenClaw. Not just a chatbot — a full system. A Trello board with 10 columns. Two modes: Fire (pure execution, one step at a time, "you have 7 minutes") and Compass (brainstorming, strategy, planning). Scheduled rituals: daily planning at 9 AM, weekly planning on Sunday, monthly grooming.

The first time I did an end-of-day brain dump — just talked into my phone at 9:43 PM, rattling off 12 tasks across insurance calls, LinkedIn posts, API work, and Telegram replies — and woke up to a perfectly organized schedule? That's when I realized: this system works. And if it works for me, it works for anyone.

That's TMF.

## The Long Version (for landing page or YouTube)

### Act 1: The Chaos

Picture this: a desk covered in sticky notes. Not ten. Not twenty. Three months' worth. The same tasks written down over and over — "fix the leaderboard," "post on LinkedIn," "call the insurance company." I wrote them to get them out of my head. But they kept coming back. Because writing them down didn't mean they got done. It just meant I had a bigger pile of paper.

I'm running four things at once: a staffing company that grosses $750K/year (but I'm the only person doing everything — sales, PM, engineering, client calls), a startup, a passion project with developers in multiple countries, and trying to build a personal brand on LinkedIn. Four kids. New house. $5K/month mortgage.

And here's the thing that really got me: I felt like I was always working. Always. But my bank account said otherwise. I was living paycheck to paycheck at $250K/year. Where was the time going? Was I actually building my business — or was I spending all my energy on hobbies and side projects while the income-generating work sat untouched?

I had no way to know. No factual picture. Just a feeling and a pile of sticky notes.

### Act 2: The Modes

One morning I sat down with my AI assistant — I'd been tinkering with OpenClaw for weeks — and had a breakthrough. I realized that my problem wasn't just organization. It was mode-switching.

Some days I needed to think strategically. What should I focus on this week? Is the Skool community worth another try? Should I hire another engineer?

Other days I just needed someone to tell me what to do next. No choices. No analysis paralysis. Just: "Do this. You have 7 minutes. Go."

I called them **Fire** and **Compass**.

**Fire Mode** is like having an army coach in your ear. One step. Timed. No options. You already decided what matters during Compass mode — now you execute. After each step: "3 of 7 done. Crushing it."

**Compass Mode** is the thinking space. Ask questions. Weigh pros and cons. Make strategic decisions. Plan the week. This is where I figured out that hiring one engineer adds $1K/month to my income — and I'd been putting off hiring for six weeks because of "dread."

### Act 3: The System

Then we built the board. Not just a to-do list — a pipeline. Raw brain dumps go into an Inbox. They get sorted into backlogs by project area. On Sunday night, I pick what matters this week. Every morning, the AI pulls my daily focus items. I work through them in Fire mode. Done items get reviewed, archived monthly.

Every task has metadata: which project, what type, estimated time, actual time. For the first time, I could see — with data — where my focus actually went.

The "hungry tickets" insight was the biggest one. Some tasks kept appearing on my sticky notes week after week. That frequency IS the priority signal. If I've written "leaderboard" seven times in three months, my subconscious is screaming at me. The system tracks that now.

### Act 4: The Product Moment

I was in the middle of setting up the cron job for my weekly planning session when it hit me:

> "I really like how you call it a framework and system. Now I really want to wrap it into a product."

OpenClaw is the most viral open-source AI project right now. 157K+ GitHub stars. Every technical person I know is playing with it. And they're all telling their non-technical friends: "You need this."

But their non-technical friends can't set it up. It requires Docker, CLI, API keys, VPS provisioning. The gap between "I heard about it" and "I use it daily" is enormous.

That's TMF. I built the system for myself. Now I'm packaging it for everyone else.
